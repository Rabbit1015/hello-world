Aortic Surgery

What we have created

Room Preparation
  • 3 transducer setup with 2 arterial line tubing sets and extra stopcock
  • Screen configuration should be “8 wave, 3 pressure overlap”. Display ABP (scale 120), PAC (scale 60) and CVP (scale 60)
  • Only some cases will require PAC – please check with the Anesthesiologist
  • 2 temperature channels: core and nasopharyngeal
  • Kimberly Clarke – large pad
  • Cerebral oximetry
  • 2 fluid warmers, 2 transfusion sets, both with LR
  • Special drugs: DDAVP, extra protamine, methylprednisolone 1,000mg
Preoperative Considerations
  • The CT Surgery, Anesthesiology and Hematology attendings will communicate before the operation to confirm the plan
  • Initial blood products that will be set up include: 6 PRBC, 6 FFP (8 for “high risk” patients1) and 3 units of PLT
  • FFP and PRBCs will be available in the OR in a cooler or the OR refrigerator as soon as a decision to proceed with the case is made
  • Platelets will become available during CPB (or after incision is made for non-cardiac cases) and will be kept in the OR on an agitator. The agitator can be found in OR 1
Intraoperative Management
  • Right and left arterial lines
  • MAC
  • Methylprednisolone 1gm IV immediately post-induction
  • CPB management protocol by Perfusion Services
  • Transfusion management according to the algorithm below
  • For antegrade cerebral perfusion (ACP) period: use right arterial line to monitor cerebral perfusion pressure; target is 50mm Hg. Begin with 5mL/kg of flow, then increments of 5mL/kg until target is reached, max 15mL/kg.

ICD's/Pacemakers

Authors: Ryan Konoske MD and Atilio Barbeito MD MPH

POC: Ryan Konoske MD

Created: August 2014 Last updated: October 2014

Important things to note if patient has a cardiac implantable electronic device (CIED)

Check EP/Cardiology Note for the following information:
  • Indication for device placement
  • Device type (pacemaker or ICD), manufacturer, and model
  • Is the patient pacemaker dependent?
  • Current programing
  • Device response to magnet placement and underlying rhythm
  • Battery longevity (should be > 3 months)
  • Any leads placed within the last 3 months?
  • Last pacing thresholds
  • Any alert status on the device (such as manufacturing recalls)

The following recommendations apply when electromagnetic interference (EMI) is expected above the umbilicus (see figure):

ICD management and response to magnet

  • All modern ICDs have pacemaker functions as well
  • Disable anti-tachycardia functions (defibrillation) prior to surgery by one of the below methods:
    • 1) Reprogramming device allows to disable defibrillation and to change pacing mode if warranted (see pacemaker management)
    • 2) Placing a magnet on an ICD will disable defibrillation, but WILL NOT ALTER PACING SETTINGS
    • If magnet is used to disable the antitachycardia function and the patient is pacemaker dependent, then reprogram the device to an asynchronous mode
  • Place external defibrillator pads on patient prior to disabling the antitachycardia function. Avoid placing pads directly over device
  • If a magnet is placed, simply remove from patient to re-activate if needed to defibrillate. This is preferable to external defibrillation for concern of damage to CIED in the event of a shock

Pacemaker management and response to magnet

  • Pacemaker dependency is important in making the decision to reprogram to asynchronous mode or to place a magnet
  • If patient is pacemaker dependent: reprogram the device to an asynchronous mode (VOO or DOO depending on single vs dual chamber pacemakers) to avoid EMI causing inhibition of pacing. This can generally be accomplished by placing a magnet over the device
  • If patient is not dependent: consider leaving in a synchronous pacing mode (DDD or VVI depending on single vs dual chamber pacemakers), and have magnet available in the OR
  • If EMI is obscuring the ECG, either the plethysmographic or intra-arterial pressure waveforms can help determine if the pacer is being inhibited (if left in synchronous mode).

Emergency Cases

  • Determine whether the device is an ICD or a pacemaker. If the patient is unaware and no records are available, a CXR may be used. ICD leads have shock coils toward the distal end, which are brighter and thicker on an x-ray.
  • Obtain a 12 lead ECG to determine whether the patient is pacemaker dependent or not. If pacing spikes are noted in front of most QRS complexes, one can assume the patient is pacemaker dependent.
